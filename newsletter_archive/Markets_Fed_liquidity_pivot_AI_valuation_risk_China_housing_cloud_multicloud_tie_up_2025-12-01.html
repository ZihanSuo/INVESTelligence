Markets: Fed liquidity pivot, AI valuation risk, China housing, cloud multicloud tie-up

Date: 2025-12-01 — Today’s briefing covers five developments that matter for markets and portfolios: the Fed’s end to quantitative tightening and the market’s repricing of rate cuts; valuation risks in AI-heavy mega-caps; continued weakness in China’s housing market; and a major multicloud networking tie-up from AWS and Google Cloud.

Fed ends QT; markets recalibrate

The Federal Reserve’s runoff of Treasuries and agency mortgage-backed securities ended on Dec. 1, halting a steady liquidity drain that had been compressing bank reserves and pressuring liquidity-sensitive assets. This stabilizes reserve levels and reduces a persistent headwind for bond prices. Source. Markets treated the move as a turning point: after the Fed signaled a less restrictive stance in October, the odds of near-term rate cuts climbed and risk assets rallied as funding-stress concerns eased (Cointelegraph, ETF Focus).

Market pricing: higher odds of a December cut

Futures and pricing models showed a sharp jump in the probability of a December 25-bp Fed cut (into the mid-80s%), prompting equity gains, lower Treasury yields and a softer dollar. That repricing lifts assets that benefit from easier policy but makes portfolios sensitive to a data-driven reversal — if jobs or inflation surprise hot, yields could re-price higher and expose stretched positions. Monitor upcoming U.S. inflation and payrolls prints and Fed commentary. Source, Source, Source.

AI mania: valuation risks for mega-caps

AI enthusiasm has driven extreme multiples for a small set of leaders (Nvidia, Microsoft, Alphabet). Analysts warn that a meaningful slowdown in AI capex or weaker cash flows could compress multiples materially — Goldman Sachs estimated downside to broad market valuation if AI investment slows — and commentators have flagged stretched prices and rising regulatory scrutiny. Investors should watch corporate capex guidance, cash-flow trends and regulatory developments; a pause in growth would likely mean multiple compression and wider market impact. Source, Source, Source.

China housing: uneven prices and downside risk

Private data show new-home prices ticked up modestly in November while resale (secondary) prices continued to fall, highlighting uneven demand between developer projects and the secondary market. Housing remains a central growth engine in China; weak prices weigh on household wealth, consumption and local government revenues tied to land sales. Watch resale prices, developer defaults, land-sale receipts and Beijing’s policy responses for signs the slump is contained or widening. Source, Source, Source.

Cloud: AWS and Google launch multicloud networking

AWS and Google Cloud unveiled a jointly managed multicloud networking service that provisions private, high-bandwidth links between the two providers in minutes, reducing latency and easing large data movement across clouds. The service lowers vendor lock-in for latency-sensitive workloads (AI training/inference, streaming, fintech) and shifts multicloud from concept toward mainstream adoption. Key near-term items: pricing/region availability, enterprise pilots, and competitors’ responses. Source, Source, Source.

Conclusion — implications and next steps: the Fed’s halt to QT reduces a steady liquidity drain and, combined with the market’s aggressive repricing of cuts, favors risk-on positioning but raises sensitivity to macro data. Valuation concentration in AI winners and China’s housing fragility present asymmetric downside risks; investors should prioritize liquidity, monitor earnings/capex signals and macro prints, and reassess exposure where gains are narrow or policy risk is elevated. On technology, the AWS–Google multicloud tie-up is structural: it eases data movement and reduces lock-in, which may accelerate enterprise AI deployments and shift vendor risk dynamics.
